Where do you sit on the income scale?

Wed 7 Mar 2018, 12:08 PM AEDT

The census reveals data about Australian income earners.

Where do you sit on the scale, and how do you compare to the top end of town?

In the very top bracket, earning over 156,000 a year (or $3,000 a week) are only 3.84 per cent of income earners.

The shire of Ashburton in north Western Australia has the highest proportion of top income earners, at 34.70 per cent.

The top 10 Local Government Areas for high income earners are all in Western Australia and New South Wales:

  1. Ashburton (WA) - 34.70 per cent
  2. Peppermint Grove (WA) - 29.74 per cent
  3. Mosman (NSW) - 23.81 per cent
  4. Cottesloe (WA) - 22.94 per cent
  5. Woollahra (NSW) - 22.75 per cent
  6. Nedlands (WA) - 21.36 per cent
  7. Hunters Hill (NSW) - 19.26 per cent
  8. Cambridge (WA) - 17.66 per cent
  9. Claremont (WA) - 17.60 per cent
  10. Ku-ring-gai (NSW) - 16.93 per cent

These 10 LGAs account for only 1.30 per cent of Australian income earners, but they make up 6.58 per cent of those in the top bracket.

And there are several LGAs where no one reported income in the highest bracket:

  • Hope Vale (Qld.)

These LGAs have two key things in common — they are all in regional or rural areas, and they all have much higher than average Indigenous populations, with most of them recording an Indigenous population of over 80 per cent.

They are also all home to under a thousand people.

So are people in the top bracket hard workers, or are they just lucky?

The data shows that the top income bracket typically works longer hours than any other, with 41.77 per cent working 49 hours or more a week, compared to 8.85 per cent of other income earners.

And it should be no surprise that certain occupations, including doctors and lawyers, are in the top five in the top bracket.

  1. Medical Practitioners (7.22 per cent)
  2. CEOs, General Managers and Legislators (7.19 per cent)
  3. Business Administration Managers (5.68 per cent)
  4. Construction, Distribution and Production Managers (5.68 per cent)
  5. Legal Professionals (3.68 per cent)

People in these professions make up more than a quarter (29.45 per cent) of people in the top income bracket.

By contrast, the top five professions make up xx per cent in your bracket.

61.79 per cent of top income earners have at least a bachelor degree and above, compared with 23.41 per cent of the rest of income earners.

And the tasks performed in jobs by people in the top bracket are less likely to be automated, according to data from economic modelling firm AlphaBeta.

In the top bracket, tasks less likely to be performed by machines comprise 65.9 per cent of tasks performed by people in that bracket. For other income earners, the number is 52.22 per cent.

So we've looked at the work aspect, what about the characteristics we are born with, or can't change?

The vast majority of top income earners are male (75.17 per cent), compared to 48.62 per cent of other income earners.

Indigenous people are greatly underrepresented, making up 0.82 per cent of the bracket, compared with 2.05 per cent in other brackets.

But being born in Australia is of little consequence to high income earners, with Australian-born workers representing 65.68 per cent in the highest bracket, and 67.05 for the rest.

The census also shows some surprising findings about this group of high-earning individuals.

High income earners are slightly more likely to do voluntary work for an organisation or group, at 29.09 per cent, compared to 20.43 for the rest.

And income doesn't seem to greatly affect the number of motor vehicles a person owns.

At 66.21 per cent, high income earners are slightly more likely to own one or two cars than the rest (64.1 per cent).

But they're slightly less likely to own three or more cars (24.04 per cent to 25.57 per cent).

Lastly, even when controlling for age, the highest income bracket has more people who are married (55.25 per cent) and less people who have never been married (33.35 per cent) than any other bracket. Its proportion of people who are divorced (5.62 per cent) is also lower when compared to all other income earners.

So perhaps money can buy love, at least, according to the census.
