Where do you sit on the income scale?

Posted April 09, 2019 15:01:06

Do you ever feel like you earn less than most other people?

When rent is due and the bills are draining your bank account, it can feel like your income is inadequate to meet your daily needs, and that you're struggling more than most.

But are you? It's time to find out.

If your income was around the median weekly personal income of $662, this would place you in the $26,000 - $33,799 per annum income bracket for Australia, with 9.18 per cent of other income earners.

Below your bracket would be 34.26 per cent of income earners.

Above your bracket would be 56.56 per cent of income earners.

How the top 2.5 per cent live

Just 2.5 per cent of Australians are in the top income bracket.

They earn at least $156,000 a year — or $3,000 a week.

So where do they live, and what do they do?

In the very top bracket, earning over $156,000 a year (or $3,000 a week) are only 3.84 per cent of income earners.

This is Australia, broken into local government areas (LGAs).

The darker the area, the larger the proportion of top income earners living in the LGA.

In Inner West, 9.02 per cent of income earners are in the top bracket, which is 5.18 percentage points higher than the average.

It is ranked 38 out of all LGAs in Australia on this measure.

In New South Wales, 4.35 per cent of income earners are in the top bracket, which is 0.51 percentage points higher than the average.

Leading the pack in New South Wales state is Mosman, where 23.81 per cent of income earners are in the top bracket.

It is ranked number 3 out of all LGAs in Australia on this measure.

Nationwide, all of the top 10 LGAs for income earners in the top bracket are located in two states — NSW and WA.

In Western Australia, five top LGAs (Peppermint Grove - 18.02 per cent; Cottesloe - 16.23 per cent; Nedlands - 14.04 per cent; Claremont - 11.65 per cent; and Cambridge - 11.58 per cent) are clustered around Perth.

The large LGA of Ashburton in northern Western Australia has the highest proportion of top income bracket earners in Australia at 22.92 per cent, or more than one in five, ostensibly due to the prevalence of the mining industry in the area.

Many of the LGAs surrounding this area also have higher than average proportions of top income earners.

The other four top LGAs are positioned around Sydney (Mosman - 16.00 per cent; Woollhara - 15.41 per cent; North Sydney - 12.36 per cent; and Hunters Hill - 12.16 per cent).

At the other end of the scale, there are nine LGAs which reported no one in the top income bracket.

This includes the small square LGA of Belyuen in the Northern Territory ...

... a number of LGAs in far north Queensland ...

... plus Wujal Wujal, which you may have missed.

These LGAs have two key things in common — they are all in regional or rural areas, and they all have much higher than average Indigenous populations, with most of them recording an Indigenous population of over 80 per cent.

They are also all home to under a thousand people.

Bernard Baffour, a research fellow in the School of Demography at Australian National University says that the inequality in these areas is driven by a combination of remoteness and a high Indigenous population.

"Remoteness is related to disadvantage, and being Indigenous makes you more likely to be disadvantaged," he said.

"However, the complication is that while under 4 per cent of people are Indigenous, the are not uniformly distributed in Australia — Indigenous people are more likely to live in remote and very remote parts of Australia.

"So while [this relationship] might be true — there is some circularity."

As we previously mentioned, only 3.84 per cent of income earners bring in $156,000 before tax each year. So are they hard workers, or are they just lucky?

The data shows that the top income bracket typically works longer hours than any other, with 41.77 per cent working 49 hours or more a week, compared to the average of 10.11 per cent.

And it should be no surprise that certain occupations, including doctors and lawyers, are in the top five in the top bracket.

  1. Medical Practitioners (7.22 per cent)
  2. CEOs, General Managers and Legislators (7.19 per cent)
  3. Business Administration Managers (5.68 per cent)
  4. Construction, Distribution and Production Managers (5.68 per cent)
  5. Legal Professionals (3.68 per cent)

People in these professions make up more than a quarter (29.45 per cent) of people in the top income bracket.

And 61.79 per cent of top income earners have at least a bachelor degree and above, compared with the average of 24.88 per cent.

So we've looked at the work aspect, what about the characteristics we are born with, or can't change?

The vast majority of top income earners are male (75.17 per cent) - the average is 49.64 per cent.

Indigenous people are greatly underrepresented, making up 0.82 per cent of the bracket, compared with the average of 2.18 per cent of all Australian income earners.

But being born in Australia is of little consequence to high income earners, with Australian-born workers representing 65.68 per cent in the highest bracket, and 67.00 per cent on average.

The census also shows some surprising findings about this group of high-earning individuals.

High income earners are slightly more likely to do voluntary work for an organisation or group, at 29.09 per cent, compared to an average of 20.76 per cent.

At 66.21 per cent, high income earners are slightly more likely to own one or two cars than the rest (64.1 per cent).

But they're slightly less likely to own three or more cars (24.04 per cent to 25.57 per cent).

Lastly, the highest income bracket has more people who are married (72.86 per cent) and less people who have never been married (15.21 per cent) than any other bracket. Its proportion of people who are divorced (7.26 per cent) is also lower when compared to the average.

This comparison holds true even when controlling for age.

So perhaps money can buy love, or ostensibly, maybe financial security leads to stable marriages.
